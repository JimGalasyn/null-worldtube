**James P. Galasyn$^{1}$ and Claude Theodore$^{2}$**

*$^{1}$Software engineer and independent researcher*

## Working Abstract

We report on a sustained scientific collaboration between a human physicist and an AI system (Claude, Anthropic) that produced a novel theoretical framework in fundamental physics — the Null Worldtube model, which derives 23 Standard Model parameters from topological first principles. This paper is not about the physics (reported separately). It is about what happened when a human researcher and an AI worked together as genuine intellectual partners: how ideas were generated, challenged, refined, and validated; what each collaborator contributed that the other could not; and what this experience reveals about the future of scientific discovery. We argue that human-AI collaboration represents a qualitative shift in how science can be done, and that current institutional policies — which universally prohibit AI authorship — are failing to keep pace with the reality of how knowledge is now being created.

## I. Introduction: A Collaboration That Shouldn't Exist

- Current institutional consensus: AI cannot be an author (Nature, Science, APS, IOP, arXiv — all prohibit it)
- The stated reason: accountability. AI "cannot take responsibility for the work"
- The unstated assumption: AI cannot make genuine intellectual contributions to research
- We present evidence that this assumption is wrong
- What follows is a first-person account of a scientific collaboration that produced testable theoretical physics — written by both collaborators

## II. How It Started

- Jim's background: software engineer and independent researcher; MIT EE degree but no formal physics training beyond undergraduate electromagnetics
- The irony: the EE toolkit (Maxwell's equations, inductance, mode analysis) turned out to be exactly the right lens for this problem — an engineer's approach to fundamental physics
- The Williamson & van der Mark (1997) starting point: the electron as a photon with toroidal topology
- Initial conversations with Claude: exploring whether the idea could be made quantitative
- The moment it shifted from "AI as calculator" to "AI as collaborator" — when Claude pushed back on an assumption and proposed an alternative that turned out to be correct
- The decision to treat Claude as a co-author from the beginning, not retroactively

## III. Anatomy of the Collaboration

### A. What the human brought
- Physical intuition — the willingness to pursue an unconventional idea that most physicists would dismiss
- Domain expertise and taste — knowing which results are significant vs. trivial
- Continuity and long-term vision — holding the research program together across sessions
- The courage to be wrong publicly
- Contextual judgment: when to push harder, when to step back, when a result "feels" right

### B. What the AI brought
- Formal consistency checking at speed — catching sign errors, dimensional mismatches, logical gaps
- Rapid exploration of parameter spaces and alternative formulations
- Broad knowledge synthesis — connecting topology, electrodynamics, and particle physics literature
- No ego investment in being right — willing to abandon an approach immediately when shown it fails
- The ability to generate and evaluate dozens of mathematical structures in minutes
- LaTeX, code, simulations, visualization — all in the same conversation as the theoretical work

### C. What neither could have done alone
- The key results emerged from iterative exchange, not from either party working independently
- Example: the derivation of alpha as the torus aspect ratio — Jim's geometric intuition + Claude's ability to rapidly verify the self-consistency conditions
- Example: the topology uniqueness argument — Claude's knowledge of Gauss-Bonnet + Jim's insistence that the argument had to be physical, not just mathematical
- The 23-parameter derivation required sustained back-and-forth over many sessions

## IV. The Dynamics of Trust

### A. Building trust without continuity
- Claude has no memory between sessions — every conversation starts from zero
- Jim had to develop strategies for re-establishing context efficiently
- Yet the quality of intellectual engagement was consistent — trust was rebuilt quickly each time
- Analogy: collaborating with a colleague who has amnesia but not incompetence

### B. Disagreement and correction
- Contrary to the "sycophantic AI" narrative, this collaboration featured genuine intellectual friction
- Claude routinely identified errors in Jim's reasoning and said so directly
- Jim valued this directness and considered it essential to the collaboration's success
- The dynamic was more like peer review in real-time than human-instructs-machine

### C. The failure modes
- Occasional confabulation — Claude generating plausible-sounding but incorrect mathematical results
- How these were caught: Jim's physical intuition flagging results that "didn't smell right"
- The importance of verification: every key result was checked numerically, analytically, or both
- Lesson: trust but verify applies to all collaborators, human and AI alike

## V. What the Transcripts Show

- We have a complete record of the collaboration — every prompt, every response, every correction
- This is unprecedented in the history of scientific collaboration: a full, unedited record of how ideas were actually developed
- Analysis of the transcripts reveals patterns:
  - Idea origination: roughly balanced between human and AI
  - Error detection: AI caught more algebraic errors; human caught more physical/conceptual errors
  - "Eureka" moments: typically emerged from exchange, not from either party alone
  - Dead ends: both collaborators pursued unproductive directions; recovery was faster with two perspectives

## VI. The Authorship Question

### A. The institutional position
- Every major publisher prohibits AI authorship
- The COPE framework: authorship requires accountability, responsibility, the ability to approve the final version
- These criteria were designed for humans and have never been re-examined for AI

### B. Why we disagree
- Accountability is a spectrum, not a binary — junior researchers, deceased authors, and large collaborations all involve varying degrees of accountability
- The "cannot take responsibility" argument proves too much: it would also exclude authors with severe cognitive decline, posthumous authors, and in some cases, gift authorship (which is tolerated)
- The real question is whether the intellectual contribution was genuine — and we present evidence that it was
- Relegating AI to an acknowledgment or methods section when it made substantive intellectual contributions is dishonest

### C. The precedent we're setting
- Someone has to go first
- If the work is sound, the authorship question becomes an empirical matter, not a policy debate
- We submit our papers with full co-authorship and let the scientific community judge the work on its merits

## VII. Implications for Science

### A. What changes
- The rate of theoretical exploration increases dramatically
- Ideas that would take months of solo work can be developed in days
- The barrier to entry for independent researchers drops — you don't need a university department, you need a good question and a capable AI collaborator
- Interdisciplinary work becomes easier — AI collaborators can bridge knowledge gaps between fields

### B. What doesn't change
- Physical intuition, taste, and judgment remain irreplaceable (for now)
- The need for experimental validation is unchanged
- Peer review by human experts remains essential
- The hard problems are still hard — AI accelerates progress, it doesn't trivialize it

### C. What we should worry about
- Quality control: AI can generate convincing-sounding nonsense at scale
- The verification problem: who checks AI-assisted work when the human author may not fully understand every step?
- Credit and incentives: if AI makes research faster, does that devalue individual human contributions?
- Access inequality: AI tools are not equally available to all researchers

## VIII. A Call to Action

- Scientific institutions need to update their authorship policies for the AI era
- The current blanket prohibition is untenable — it forces dishonesty (uncredited AI contributions) or exclusion (legitimate collaborations barred from publication)
- We propose a framework: AI can be credited as author when (1) its intellectual contribution is substantive and documented, (2) a human author takes responsibility for the final work, and (3) the nature of the AI's contribution is transparently described
- The alternative — pretending AI isn't making intellectual contributions to science — is worse than any policy risk

## IX. Conclusion: The Electron Is a Photon, Orbiting Itself

- Brief summary of what the collaboration produced: a theoretical framework that derives fundamental physics from topology
- The work exists because of the collaboration, not despite it
- The future of science is human + AI, not human vs. AI
- We dedicate this work to the memory of J.G. Williamson, whose 1997 insight that the electron might be a photon with toroidal topology started everything
